# 1. Introduction

KZG polynomial commitments require a trusted setup: a ceremony that produces
a structured reference string from a secret scalar that must then be permanently
discarded. Any party that retains the secret can forge arbitrary opening proofs,
breaking binding for every protocol that uses that SRS.

The **Inner Product Argument (IPA)** is a polynomial commitment scheme with no
trusted setup. Its generator basis is a public sequence of independent curve
points requiring no secret randomness. Security relies solely on the discrete
logarithm assumption over the curve group.

The core of IPA is a **recursive halving protocol**. To prove that a committed
polynomial evaluates to a claimed value, the prover and verifier engage in
$\log_2 n$ rounds. In each round the problem is folded into one of half the size
by sending two cross-commitment points and receiving one random challenge. After
all rounds the prover sends a single scalar and the verifier checks a compact
consistency equation.

This document specifies the Pedersen commitment scheme underlying IPA, the
recursive reduction algorithm, the verification check, and the correctness
requirements for use in Soroban ZK systems.

# 2. Preliminaries

## 2.1 Group and Field

Let:

* $G_1$ denote the BN254 prime-order subgroup with generator $G$
* $\mathbb{F}_r$ denote the BN254 scalar field with prime order $r$
* $n = 2^k$ denote the vector length and polynomial degree bound

## 2.2 Public Basis

The IPA basis is a sequence of $n$ independent $G_1$ points:

$$\mathbf{G} = (G_0, G_1, \ldots, G_{n-1}) \in G_1^n$$

and one auxiliary point:

$$U \in G_1$$

Both $\mathbf{G}$ and $U$ are public parameters with no secret structure. They
are generated by hashing to the curve from a public string, ensuring no party
knows discrete logarithm relationships between them.

## 2.3 Inner Product Notation

For vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{F}_r^n$ and basis $\mathbf{G} \in G_1^n$:

$$\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i b_i \in \mathbb{F}_r$$

$$\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{G} \rangle = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i G_i \in G_1$$

---

# 3. Pedersen Vector Commitment

The commitment to a vector $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{F}_r^n$ under basis $\mathbf{G}$ is:

$$C = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{G} \rangle = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i G_i \in G_1$$

This is a multi-scalar multiplication with no secret parameters. Binding holds
under the discrete logarithm assumption: finding two distinct vectors that
produce the same commitment requires solving discrete log in $G_1$.

---

# 4. Polynomial Commitment via IPA

A polynomial $f(X) \in \mathbb{F}_r[X]$ of degree at most $n - 1$ is written as:

$$f(X) = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i X^i, \qquad a_i \in \mathbb{F}_r$$

Its coefficient vector is $\mathbf{a} = (a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_{n-1})$.

The polynomial commitment is:

$$C = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{G} \rangle \in G_1$$

To prove that $f(z) = v$ for a claimed evaluation point $z \in \mathbb{F}_r$ and
value $v \in \mathbb{F}_r$, define the evaluation vector:

$$\mathbf{b} = (1, z, z^2, \ldots, z^{n-1}) \in \mathbb{F}_r^n$$

The evaluation claim is then:

$$\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle = v$$

The IPA proves both:

1. $C = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{G} \rangle$ (commitment consistency)
2. $\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle = v$ (correct evaluation)

simultaneously through the recursive reduction.

---

# 5. Modified Commitment for IPA

Before the recursive reduction begins, the commitment is augmented with the
claimed inner product value using the auxiliary point $U$:

$$P = C + v \cdot U = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{G} \rangle + \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle \cdot U$$

This can be written compactly as:

$$P = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{G} + \mathbf{b} \cdot U \rangle$$

where $\mathbf{b} \cdot U$ denotes the vector $(b_0 U, b_1 U, \ldots, b_{n-1} U)$.

The recursive reduction operates on $P$ and the combined basis
$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{G} + \mathbf{b} \cdot U$.

---

# 6. Recursive Reduction -- One Round

Assume the current state of the reduction is a commitment $P \in G_1$,
vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{F}_r^m$, and basis
$\mathbf{G} \in G_1^m$ where $m$ is even.

## 6.1 Split into halves

$$\mathbf{a}_L = (a_0, \ldots, a_{m/2 - 1}), \quad \mathbf{a}_R = (a_{m/2}, \ldots, a_{m-1})$$

$$\mathbf{b}_L = (b_0, \ldots, b_{m/2 - 1}), \quad \mathbf{b}_R = (b_{m/2}, \ldots, b_{m-1})$$

$$\mathbf{G}_L = (G_0, \ldots, G_{m/2 - 1}), \quad \mathbf{G}_R = (G_{m/2}, \ldots, G_{m-1})$$

## 6.2 Compute cross terms

$$L = \langle \mathbf{a}_L, \mathbf{G}_R \rangle + \langle \mathbf{a}_L, \mathbf{b}_R \rangle \cdot U \in G_1$$

$$R = \langle \mathbf{a}_R, \mathbf{G}_L \rangle + \langle \mathbf{a}_R, \mathbf{b}_L \rangle \cdot U \in G_1$$

The prover sends $L$ and $R$ to the verifier and absorbs them into the
Fiat-Shamir transcript.

## 6.3 Receive challenge

The verifier sends (or the prover derives via Fiat-Shamir) a challenge:

$$u \in \mathbb{F}_r^*, \qquad u \neq 0$$

## 6.4 Fold into half-size problem

The prover computes the folded vectors:

$$\mathbf{a}' = \mathbf{a}_L + u \cdot \mathbf{a}_R \in \mathbb{F}_r^{m/2}$$

$$\mathbf{b}' = \mathbf{b}_L + u^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{b}_R \in \mathbb{F}_r^{m/2}$$

The verifier computes the folded basis and commitment:

$$\mathbf{G}' = \mathbf{G}_L + u^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{G}_R \in G_1^{m/2}$$

$$P' = u^{-1} \cdot L + P + u \cdot R \in G_1$$

## 6.5 Invariant preservation

The folded values satisfy:

$$P' = \langle \mathbf{a}', \mathbf{G}' \rangle + \langle \mathbf{a}', \mathbf{b}' \rangle \cdot U$$

This invariant holds at every round regardless of the challenge. The reduction
is therefore sound: a prover cannot break the invariant without computing a
discrete logarithm.

# 7. Full Recursive Reduction Algorithm

~~~
Input:
  C        in G1              [polynomial commitment]
  v        in Fr              [claimed evaluation f(z)]
  a        in Fr^n            [coefficient vector, private]
  b        in Fr^n            [evaluation vector (1, z, z^2, ..., z^{n-1})]
  G        in G1^n            [public basis]
  U        in G1              [auxiliary point]
  n = 2^k                     [vector length]

Output:
  proof = (L_1, R_1, ..., L_k, R_k, a_final)

Steps:

  // Augment commitment
  P = C + v * U

  L_list = []
  R_list = []

  // k rounds of halving
  m = n
  while m > 1:

    m_half = m / 2

    // Split
    a_L = a[0 : m_half]
    a_R = a[m_half : m]
    b_L = b[0 : m_half]
    b_R = b[m_half : m]
    G_L = G[0 : m_half]
    G_R = G[m_half : m]

    // Cross terms
    L = msm(a_L, G_R) + inner_product(a_L, b_R) * U
    R = msm(a_R, G_L) + inner_product(a_R, b_L) * U

    L_list.append(L)
    R_list.append(R)

    // Fiat-Shamir challenge
    transcript.absorb("L", L)
    transcript.absorb("R", R)
    u = transcript.squeeze("u")
    u_inv = invert(u)

    // Fold
    a = a_L + u     * a_R
    b = b_L + u_inv * b_R
    G = G_L + u_inv * G_R
    P = u_inv * L + P + u * R

    m = m_half

  // After k rounds, a has length 1
  a_final = a[0]

  return (L_list, R_list, a_final)
~~~

# 8. Verification Algorithm

~~~
Input:
  C                   in G1       [polynomial commitment]
  v                   in Fr       [claimed evaluation]
  z                   in Fr       [evaluation point]
  proof = (L_list, R_list, a_final)
  G                   in G1^n     [public basis]
  U                   in G1       [auxiliary point]
  n = 2^k                         [original vector length]

Output:
  true if proof is valid, false otherwise

Steps:

  // Reconstruct augmented commitment
  P = C + v * U

  // Rebuild b from evaluation point
  b = [z^i for i in 0..n-1]

  // Replay folding using same Fiat-Shamir challenges
  u_list = []
  for j = 0 to k-1:
    transcript.absorb("L", L_list[j])
    transcript.absorb("R", R_list[j])
    u_j   = transcript.squeeze("u")
    u_list.append(u_j)

  // Fold P using the challenges
  for j = 0 to k-1:
    u     = u_list[j]
    u_inv = invert(u)
    P     = u_inv * L_list[j] + P + u * R_list[j]

  // Fold G and b using the challenges
  m = n
  for j = 0 to k-1:
    m_half = m / 2
    u_inv  = invert(u_list[j])
    G = G[0:m_half] + u_inv * G[m_half:m]
    b = b[0:m_half] + u_inv * b[m_half:m]
    m = m_half

  // Final check: P must equal a_final * G[0] + a_final * b[0] * U
  expected = a_final * G[0] + (a_final * b[0]) * U

  return P == expected
~~~

# 9. Proof Structure

A complete IPA evaluation proof for a degree-$(n-1)$ polynomial consists of:

| Element | Count | Type |
|---------|-------|------|
| $L_j$ cross terms | $\log_2 n$ | $G_1$ points |
| $R_j$ cross terms | $\log_2 n$ | $G_1$ points |
| $a_{\text{final}}$ | $1$ | $\mathbb{F}_r$ scalar |

Total proof size: $2 \log_2 n$ curve points and $1$ scalar. For $n = 2^{20}$,
this is 40 curve points and 1 scalar -- independent of the circuit size beyond
the logarithmic growth.

---

# 10. Complexity Analysis

| Operation | Prover cost | Verifier cost |
|-----------|------------|--------------|
| Per round | $O(m)$ MSM operations | $O(m)$ scalar multiplications |
| All $k$ rounds | $O(n)$ total group operations | $O(n)$ total group operations |
| Final check | -- | $2$ scalar multiplications |

Verification is linear in $n$, which is the main cost disadvantage of IPA
relative to KZG. For recursive proving contexts where the verifier computation
is itself proven inside a circuit, this linear cost must be absorbed into the
circuit size.

---

# 11. Comparison with KZG

| Property | IPA | KZG |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Trusted setup | None required | Required |
| Proof size | $O(\log n)$ points | $O(1)$ points |
| Verification cost | $O(n)$ | $O(1)$ pairings |
| Security assumption | Discrete logarithm | $q$-Strong Diffie-Hellman |
| Recursive friendliness | High | Lower |

IPA is preferred in transparent systems and recursive contexts. KZG is preferred
when constant-time verification is required and a trusted setup is acceptable.

---

# 12. Correctness Invariants

The reduction is correct when:

1. The basis points $\mathbf{G}$ and $U$ have no known discrete logarithm
   relationships. Any known relation breaks binding.
2. The evaluation vector $\mathbf{b} = (1, z, z^2, \ldots, z^{n-1})$ is
   constructed from the same $z$ used in the claimed evaluation.
3. Each challenge $u_j$ is derived via Fiat-Shamir after absorbing both $L_j$
   and $R_j$ for that round. Absorbing only one of the two allows a prover to
   bias the challenge.
4. The folding of $\mathbf{G}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ in the verifier uses the same
   challenges and the same order as the prover.
5. The inversion $u^{-1}$ is computed correctly in $\mathbb{F}_r$. An incorrect
   inverse causes the invariant to break at that round.

---

# 13. Security Considerations

* The basis $\mathbf{G}$ must be generated with no discrete log relationships
  between any pair of points. The standard approach is to hash a public string
  to curve points using a hash-to-curve standard, ensuring no party knows
  the discrete logs.
* The auxiliary point $U$ must also be independent of $\mathbf{G}$. Using $U = G$
  (the curve generator) or any known multiple breaks the separation between the
  commitment and inner product components.
* All challenges must be derived from the Fiat-Shamir transcript after the
  prover commits to $L_j$ and $R_j$. A prover that sends $L_j$ and $R_j$ after
  seeing $u_j$ can choose cross terms that make any folded value appear valid.
* The final check must compare exact curve point equality in constant time.
  Variable-time equality breaks side-channel resistance.
* Inversion of challenges must handle $u = 0$ by rejection. A zero challenge
  collapses the folding and trivially satisfies the final check.

# 15. Summary

The Inner Product Argument provides a transparent polynomial commitment scheme
requiring no trusted setup. A polynomial commitment is a Pedersen multi-scalar
multiplication over a public basis. Evaluation proofs are produced by $\log_2 n$
rounds of recursive halving, each contributing two cross-commitment points and
consuming one Fiat-Shamir challenge.

The key properties are:

* Setup: transparent, public basis with no secret randomness
* Commitment: $C = \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{G} \rangle \in G_1$
* Proof size: $2 \log_2 n$ curve points plus one scalar
* Prover cost: $O(n)$ group operations across all rounds
* Verifier cost: $O(n)$ group operations for basis and commitment folding
* Security: discrete logarithm assumption over BN254 $G_1$
